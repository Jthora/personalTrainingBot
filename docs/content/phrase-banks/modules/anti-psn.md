## Narcissistic Abuse Patterns

**keywords**: narcissistic, abuse, patterns, cycle, idealize, devalue, discard, NPD, narcissism

### Facts
- The narcissistic abuse cycle has three phases: idealization (love-bombing, putting the target on a pedestal, intense positive attention), devaluation (criticism, contempt, withdrawal, rage), and discard (abrupt end, often followed by replacement with a new target and triangulation).
- Idealization is not genuine regard — it is the projection of an idealized image onto the target. When the target inevitably fails to inhabit that projection, the devaluation phase begins.
- Narcissistic injury refers to the disproportionate rage or withdrawal that follows perceived criticism, humiliation, or failure to provide the expected admiration (narcissistic supply). The magnitude of the response is often wildly disproportionate to the trigger.
- Flying monkeys are third parties recruited by the abuser to pressure, surveil, or harass the target. They may be unwitting pawns who believe they are helping, or willing participants who benefit from the abuser's approval.

### Procedures
- Document the abuse cycle over time: use a dated log to identify when idealization shifted to devaluation. The pattern (not isolated incidents) is the evidence. A single incident is ambiguous; a cyclical pattern is diagnostic.
- Flying monkey identification: when pressure to return to the relationship comes through a third party, document: what they were told about the situation, whether they sought information from both parties, and what they are being asked to do.

### Exercises
- Map a relationship you are analyzing onto the three-phase abuse cycle. Identify specific events that mark transitions between phases. Identify how long each phase lasted.
- Research the difference between narcissistic personality disorder (clinical diagnosis) and narcissistic abuse patterns (behavioral description). Clarify why the behavioral description is operationally useful even when a diagnosis is unavailable.

## DARVO

**keywords**: DARVO, deny, attack, reverse, victim, offender, accountability, deflection

### Facts
- DARVO (coined by Jennifer Freyd) stands for: Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender. It is the characteristic response pattern of an abuser when confronted with accountability.
- Deny: the abusive behavior is denied, minimized, or reframed. "That didn't happen," "You're exaggerating," "I was only joking."
- Attack: the person raising the concern is attacked — their character, motives, mental stability, or credibility. This shifts the conversation from the abuser's behavior to the target's reliability.
- Reverse Victim and Offender: the abuser positions themselves as the real victim. "You're hurting me by saying this," "I can't believe you'd accuse me of that," "Do you know what you've done to me?"
- DARVO is effective because it is emotionally overwhelming: the target is simultaneously managing their own distress, defending against character attack, and processing the abuser's claimed victimhood. DARVO works by quantity, not quality of response.

### Procedures
- When documenting a DARVO response: log the specific behavior being addressed (the predicate), then log each of the three components (Deny / Attack / Reverse-Victim) with direct quotes where possible. The pattern becomes visible in the record.
- Counter-DARVO: return to the specific behavior repeatedly. Name the pattern explicitly: "I notice you're not addressing what I said and are now describing yourself as the injured party." Write down the predicate before any conversation to keep yourself anchored.

### Exercises
- Analyze a documented public DARVO response (deposition transcripts, recorded interviews, publicly available communications). Identify which of the three components is deployed, in what sequence, and at what intensity.
- Practice returning to the predicate: write variations of "I want to return to the specific thing I described" for use when a conversation DARVO-spirals.

## Gaslighting

**keywords**: gaslighting, reality, perception, manipulation, memory, doubt, crazy, making up

### Facts
- Gaslighting is the systematic undermining of a target's confidence in their own perceptions, memory, and reality. The name derives from the 1944 film Gaslight.
- Gaslighting operates through accumulated small incidents: a pattern of being told that what you saw/heard/experienced is incorrect, exaggerated, or invented. Individual incidents seem trivial; the cumulative effect is severe self-doubt.
- Gaslighting is particularly effective against people who were already taught to distrust their own perceptions (childhood trauma, prior abusive relationships, high-control group conditioning).
- Gaslighting serves both a control function (a person who doubts their perceptions cannot effectively organize against an abuser) and a cover function (a person who doubts their perceptions is less credible to outside observers).

### Procedures
- When gaslighting is suspected, externalize your perception record: write down events, conversations, and your perceptions of them before they are reframed. The written record becomes an anchor when your memory or perception is subsequently disputed.
- Distinguish between genuine disagreement (two valid perceptions of a shared event that differ) and gaslighting (systematic, consistent, pattern-form denial of the target's perceptions across topics and incidents).

### Exercises
- Identify three specific gaslighting phrases and the reality-undermining mechanism of each: ("You're too sensitive," "That never happened," "You always misremember," "You're being paranoid"). For each, write the function it serves for the person using it.
- Design a perception-recording system for someone in a situation where gaslighting is occurring. Specify what to record, when, and where to store it securely.

## Coercive Control

**keywords**: coercive, control, isolation, financial, monitoring, surveillance, autonomy, stalking, intimidation

### Facts
- Coercive control (Evan Stark's framework, also a criminal offense in UK/Scotland) is a pattern of behavior that seeks to take away the victim's liberty or freedom and strip away their sense of self.
- The five domains of coercive control: isolation (from family, friends, social networks), monitoring and surveillance (of communications, location, social interactions), deprivation (of financial resources, sleep, food, medical care), microregulation (controlling clothing, diet, movement, relationships), and threat and intimidation.
- Coercive control is often more psychologically damaging than physical violence and is the strongest predictor of lethal intimate partner violence.
- Digital coercive control extends traditional patterns: coercive partners install stalkerware on devices, require constant location sharing, demand passwords and access, and maintain surveillance through social media monitoring.

### Procedures
- Coercive control assessment: systematically review all five domains against the specific relationship. Document with specific examples per domain. The pattern (all five domains) is the evidence; any single domain in isolation may have a benign explanation.
- Digital safety planning: if device compromise (stalkerware) is suspected, do not check for it using the compromised device. Use a separate, clean device or a public computer for sensitive communications and planning.

### Exercises
- Research a documented coercive control case. Map the specific behaviors to Evan Stark's five domains. Identify at which point intervention would have been most effective and what intervention would have looked like.
- Design a digital safety checklist for someone in a coercive control situation: device security, communications security, account security, and location privacy. Prioritize by operational urgency.

## Recovery from Narcissistic Abuse

**keywords**: recovery, healing, C-PTSD, trauma, bond, intermittent, reinforcement, no-contact

### Facts
- Recovery from narcissistic abuse typically involves complex post-traumatic stress responses (C-PTSD): intrusive memories, emotional flashbacks, hypervigilance, difficulty with trust, and a pervasive sense of threat even in safe environments.
- The trauma bond (sometimes called Stockholm Syndrome in popular usage) is a specific attachment pattern that forms under intermittent reinforcement: unpredictable cycles of punishment and reward create stronger psychological attachment than consistent positive reinforcement.
- Intermittent reinforcement is the most psychologically powerful reinforcement schedule — the same mechanism that drives gambling behavior. The unpredictability of positive responses creates compulsive pursuit of those responses.
- No-contact is a recovery protocol, not a punishment: it removes the ongoing reinforcement schedule that maintains the trauma bond. Partial contact (grey rock, limited contact) is sometimes used when full no-contact is not possible (shared parenting, shared workplace).

### Procedures
- When explaining the trauma bond to a survivor: use the intermittent reinforcement model. The bond is not a character failing — it is the predictable result of a specific psychological mechanism. Understanding the mechanism depathologizes the survivor's attachment.
- Grey rock method: in mandatory contact situations (shared parenting), make yourself as uninteresting as possible — short, flat, parallel responses that provide no emotional supply, no conflict, and no escalation hooks.

### Exercises
- Identify the specific intermittent reinforcement sequence in a relationship under analysis: what was unpredictably provided? At what interval? What behavior in the target did each reward reinforce?
- Research the neuroscience of trauma bonding: identify the specific hormonal/neurological mechanisms (cortisol, dopamine, oxytocin) and how they interact with intermittent reinforcement to create compulsive attachment.
