## BITE Model — Behavior Control

**keywords**: bite, behavior, control, sleep, diet, finances, time, relationships, activities

### Facts
- Behavior control in coercive organizations regulates: sleep schedules (often under 7 hours to induce mental compliance), diet and nutrition (creating physical dependence), financial autonomy (collecting income or requiring asset surrender), social contact (restricting outside relationships), and personal time allocation.
- Sleep deprivation is a deliberate destabilization tactic in high-control groups — it reduces critical thinking capacity and increases suggestibility, not an incidental side effect.
- Financial dependence is engineered through tithing requirements, unpaid labor for the group, and discouragement or prohibition of outside employment.
- Behavioral compliance is enforced through a peer surveillance system: members are rewarded for reporting non-compliant members, creating an internal intelligence network.

### Procedures
- When evaluating a group for behavior control, audit: sleep time rules, dietary restrictions with shame/punishment enforcement, financial requirements, rules on contact with outsiders, and time allocation mandates.
- Document the enforcement mechanism for each behavior rule — peer reporting, confession requirements, public shaming rituals, or financial penalties.

### Exercises
- List the behavioral rules of a group under examination. For each rule, identify whether the enforcement mechanism is external (punishment) or internal (guilt/shame conditioning).
- Interview a former member about what happened concretely when a behavioral rule was broken — distinguish between stated policy and actual enforcement.

## BITE Model — Information Control

**keywords**: information, control, media, outside, sources, critical, discourage, access

### Facts
- Information control involves restricting access to outside information sources: banning or discouraging newspapers, books, websites, or contact with former members.
- Members are taught that outside information is dangerous, contaminated, or spiritually harmful — pre-emptively discrediting critical sources before members encounter them.
- Group-generated information is presented as the only reliable source: internal publications, approved speakers, official interpretations of doctrine.
- Confession and journaling practices serve a dual function: emotional release for the member and intelligence collection for the leadership. Personal disclosures are later used as leverage.

### Procedures
- Map the information diet of a group under analysis: list approved vs. prohibited sources. The more granular the prohibition list, the more advanced the information control.
- Test for thought-stopping: ask a member a question that requires consulting an outside source. Observe whether the question is deflected, reframed, or answered with internal doctrine regardless of the question's actual content.

### Exercises
- Identify three sources of external information that a group explicitly prohibits or discourages. For each, research what that source actually contains about the group.
- Access a primary source that a group prohibits and compare its actual claims to how the group characterizes it internally.

## BITE Model — Thought Control

**keywords**: thought, control, thoughtcrime, loaded language, stopping, meditation, chanting, bad thoughts

### Facts
- Thought control is enforced through loaded language: specialized jargon that compresses complex ideas into simple slogans and prevents nuanced analysis. The vocabulary itself does the thinking.
- Thought-stopping techniques include mandated meditation, chanting, prayer, repetitive activity, or humming — any activity that interrupts critical or questioning thoughts.
- Members learn to self-police their own thoughts, labeling doubt or criticism as "bad thoughts," "the flesh," "the ego," or "negativity" — concepts requiring suppression rather than examination.
- Black-and-white thinking is installed as doctrine: people, actions, and information are categorically good/bad, saved/unsaved, awake/asleep. Nuance is a spiritual failure.

### Procedures
- When a member uses loaded language terms, ask them to define the term without using other loaded language from the group's vocabulary. Inability to do so indicates the language is doing cognitive work the person cannot do independently.
- Document the thought-stopping practices taught by the group: when members are taught to use them (during doubt, during outside information exposure, during critical thinking about leadership).

### Exercises
- Select five loaded language terms from a group's vocabulary. For each, write a definition using only general English. Identify what analysis the term forecloses.
- Practice the AI exercise: when you find yourself using a loaded language term, pause and restate the concept in two to three sentences of plain English.

## BITE Model — Emotional Control

**keywords**: emotional, control, fear, guilt, shame, happiness, expression, manipulation

### Facts
- Emotional control in coercive groups regulates which emotions are permissible: happiness and gratitude to the group/leader are encouraged; doubt, anger at leadership, grief, and sadness are suppressed or pathologized.
- Fear is the primary control mechanism: fear of leaving (shunning, spiritual damnation, family separation), fear of outside information (contamination), fear of internal doubt (spiritual failure, punishment).
- Guilt and shame are weaponized: members are made to feel guilty for normal human thoughts, needs, and past actions. This creates a permanent state of debt to the group that can only be discharged through greater compliance.
- Phobia indoctrination is a specific technique: members are taught extreme, disproportionate fear responses to leaving the group — that leaving will result in death, destruction, loss of all relationships, or eternal punishment.

### Procedures
- Map the emotional landscape of a group: list which emotions are encouraged, which are suppressed, and what happens when prohibited emotions are expressed.
- Identify phobia indoctrination: what specific catastrophic outcomes does the group teach will happen to members who leave?

### Exercises
- List three normal human emotions that a group under examination treats as dangerous or sinful. For each, document the specific teaching used to pathologize it.
- Identify a phobia installed by a group and research the base rate of that catastrophic outcome actually occurring for people who leave.

## Loaded Language

**keywords**: loaded language, jargon, thought-terminating, clichés, vocabulary, slogans

### Facts
- Loaded (thought-terminating) language is vocabulary that short-circuits analysis: a single term or phrase replaces extended reasoning. "That's your ego talking" terminates a line of inquiry without engaging its content.
- Thought-terminating clichés are recognizable because they: answer questions that weren't asked, reframe criticism as personal failure, invoke authority rather than evidence, and require no factual knowledge to deploy.
- The more extensive a group's specialized vocabulary, the more advanced the thought control system. "Thought-terminating clichés" (Robert Jay Lifton's term) are the primary delivery mechanism.
- Loaded language creates an in-group communication system. Outsiders cannot participate fluently; members cannot communicate their doubts clearly even to themselves.

### Procedures
- Catalog the loaded language vocabulary of a group under examination. For each term, document: what question it terminates, what alternative analysis it forecloses, and what action it implies.
- Test for loaded language dependency: present a member with a critical observation using only general vocabulary. Observe whether the response engages the observation or deflects to group vocabulary.

### Exercises
- Take a thought-terminating cliché used by a group. Expand it into the full argument it would need to make if stated in non-loaded language. Identify what that argument would need to defend.
- Practice counterphrase generation: for a loaded language term, author a plain-language restatement that preserves the factual content while removing the thought-stopping function.

## Coercive Persuasion

**keywords**: coercive, persuasion, milieu, control, environment, undue influence, reform, thought reform

### Facts
- Coercive persuasion (Robert Jay Lifton's "thought reform") operates through environmental control: controlling the physical environment (who you live with, sleep near, eat with) controls thought without overt force.
- The eight criteria for thought reform environments (Lifton): milieu control, mystical manipulation, demand for purity, confession, sacred science, loading the language, doctrine over person, dispensing of existence.
- Undue influence differs from persuasion: it exploits psychological vulnerabilities (exhaustion, isolation, emotional overwhelm) to bypass rational decision-making. Consent under undue influence is not meaningful consent.
- Coercive persuasion does not require physical coercion — it operates through manufactured social reality. The subject believes they are making free choices within a reality that has been engineered.

### Procedures
- Apply Lifton's eight criteria as an audit checklist. A group scores from 0–8. Scores of 5+ indicate a high-control environment. No single criterion is definitive; the pattern matters.
- When assessing a specific interaction for undue influence, evaluate the environmental conditions: Was the subject sleep-deprived? Isolated from outside relationships? Under emotional stress? Had they been told that doubt is sinful?

### Exercises
- Apply Lifton's eight criteria to a group under examination. Score each criterion from 0–2 (not present / somewhat present / fully present). Identify which criteria interact and amplify each other.
- Research one documented case of thought reform from a non-religious context (corporate cult, political movement, therapist cult). Map the specific techniques used to Lifton's criteria.

## Influence Operations

**keywords**: influence, operations, disinformation, propaganda, narrative, perception management, psyop

### Facts
- An influence operation aims to change the beliefs, attitudes, or behaviors of a target audience without their knowledge that an operation is underway.
- The three primary channels: information (what people believe to be true), emotion (how people feel about a situation), and identity (who people believe they are and who they are loyal to).
- Effective counter-influence requires identifying the operation before countering it. The primary indicators: narratives that spread unusually fast, narratives that arrive pre-packaged with emotional triggers, narratives that cannot be questioned without triggering social cost.
- Disinformation is not primarily false information — it is true information deployed in misleading context, combined with false information to contaminate the entire information space.

### Procedures
- Influence operation detection checklist: (1) WHO is spreading this narrative? (2) What reaction does it predictably provoke? (3) Who benefits if that reaction is widespread? (4) What evidence would disprove it, and is that evidence being surfaced? (5) Is questioning the narrative socially costly?
- When analyzing a specific narrative, separate the factual claims from the framing. The framing is where the manipulation usually lives.

### Exercises
- Select a narrative currently circulating in a domain you monitor. Apply the five-step detection checklist. Identify the specific emotional trigger embedded in the framing.
- Research a documented influence operation (Internet Research Agency, COINTELPRO, or similar). Map the specific techniques to the three channels (information, emotion, identity).

## Cult of Personality

**keywords**: cult, personality, leader, infallibility, charisma, special, divine, mission

### Facts
- A cult of personality is constructed through systematic attribution of special status to a leader: divine mandate, unique knowledge, special mission, or infallibility.
- The leader's personal failings are explained away using the doctrine itself: the leader's anger is righteous, their financial exploitation is divine provision, their sexual misconduct is spiritual practice or special dispensation.
- Critical thinking about the leader is made equivalent to critical thinking about the doctrine. Questioning the leader is apostasy. This conflation is deliberately constructed.
- A functioning cult of personality requires the leader to be positioned as the sole or primary channel of access to something the member genuinely needs: salvation, truth, safety, community, healing.

### Procedures
- Test for cult of personality: ask whether there is any action the leader could take that would be recognized by members as wrong. If no action can be named that would disqualify the leader, the cult of personality construction is complete.
- Document the specific mechanism by which the leader's special status was established: revelation, prophecy, track record, academic credential, or simple assertion repeated until accepted.

### Exercises
- Identify three leader behaviors that would be recognized as abusive in a non-group context. Document the specific doctrine used to reframe each behavior as acceptable or righteous.
- Research one case of leadership accountability in a high-control group. What was the sequence of events that allowed members to recognize the leader's behavior as harmful?
