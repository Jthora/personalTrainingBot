## Intelligence Cycle

**keywords**: intelligence, cycle, planning, collection, processing, analysis, dissemination, requirements

### Facts
- The intelligence cycle has five phases: (1) Planning and direction (requirements definition), (2) Collection, (3) Processing and exploitation, (4) Analysis and production, (5) Dissemination.
- The cycle is iterative, not linear: finished intelligence usually generates new collection requirements, which restart the cycle. Good analysis identifies the gaps in current intelligence rather than only answering known questions.
- Intelligence requirements must be specific and answerable. "What is the adversary's intention?" is not a requirement — it is a wish. "What communications have occurred between X and Y in the last 72 hours regarding Z?" is a requirement.
- The distinction between information and intelligence: information is raw data; intelligence is information that has been processed, contextualized, and evaluated for relevance to a specific decision.

### Procedures
- Requirements definition: before any collection activity, write out: (1) the specific question to be answered, (2) the decision that will be informed by the answer, (3) the minimum acceptable confidence level, (4) the deadline. This prevents purpose-free collection.
- Cycle review: after each intelligence product, evaluate: What collection gaps were identified? What requirements were generated for the next cycle? What assumptions were made that should be tested?

### Exercises
- Write three intelligence requirements for a specific operational scenario, formatted as specific, answerable questions. For each, identify: what collection method would address it, what confidence level is achievable, and what decision it informs.
- Analyze a historical intelligence failure (the 9/11 Commission Report, the Iraq WMD NIE, or similar). Identify at which phase of the cycle the failure occurred and what procedural change would have caught it.

## HUMINT — Human Intelligence

**keywords**: humint, human, source, asset, handler, elicitation, recruitment, reporting, validation

### Facts
- HUMINT (Human Intelligence) is intelligence derived from human sources: recruited assets, walk-ins, liaison partners, detainees, or open source interviews.
- Source validation is the critical discipline: every HUMINT report must be evaluated on (1) access — did the source have access to the information claimed?, (2) credibility — does the source have a history of reliable reporting?, (3) motivation — what does the source benefit from providing this information?, and (4) corroboration — is there independent confirmation?
- Source motivation matters because it predicts behavior under pressure: a source motivated by ideology will sustain reporting when it becomes dangerous; a source motivated by money may cease reporting or fabricate when payment is threatened.
- Walk-ins (volunteers who offer information unsolicited) require heightened validation: their motivation is unvetted, their counterintelligence exposure is unknown, and they may be provocateurs.

### Procedures
- Source assessment protocol: for every piece of HUMINT, document: Source access to the information (how did they know this?), Source reliability rating (based on track record), Currency (when was the source in position to observe this?), and corroboration (what independent evidence supports it?).
- Elicitation principle: people reveal more information in the course of a natural conversation than in response to direct questions. Elicitation uses active listening, apparent shared interest, and controlled self-disclosure to draw out information without signaling collection intent.

### Exercises
- Evaluate a historical intelligence report (declassified documents from the CREST database or equivalent) against the four-part source assessment protocol. Identify any fields that were not addressed or were addressed inadequately.
- Research a documented case where an intelligence agency's source was a double agent (FBI's Robert Hanssen, CIA's Aldrich Ames). Identify which source assessment criteria were inadequately applied and what indicators were missed.

## OSINT — Open Source Intelligence

**keywords**: osint, open-source, public, records, social-media, geolocation, imagery, research

### Facts
- OSINT is intelligence derived from publicly available information: news media, social media, government records, academic publications, commercial imagery, and public databases.
- The value of OSINT has expanded dramatically: social media posts, geotagged photographs, flight tracking services, ship AIS data, and commercial satellite imagery provide near-real-time visibility that previously required clandestine collection.
- OSINT collection is legal (by definition — the sources are public); OSINT methodology determines quality. The ability to collect everything does not mean everything collected is relevant or reliable.
- Geolocation from imagery: photographs contain embedded EXIF data (latitude, longitude, timestamp), identifiable landmarks, shadows (for sun angle and time estimation), and flora/fauna indicators. A photograph taken anywhere with a connected phone is geolocatable.

### Procedures
- OSINT research methodology: use a structured research plan before searching. Define the subject, the questions, the priority sources, and the time boundary for the search. Unstructured OSINT work produces unfocused collections.
- Source registration for OSINT: use dedicated accounts (not linked to operative identity) for social media monitoring. Do not follow or interact with targets from accounts linked to operative identity — interactions are visible and leave footprints.

### Exercises
- Using only public sources, compile a baseline OSINT profile on a public figure (politician, executive, or public institution). Document every data source, the information derived from it, and whether the information was confirmed by a second independent source.
- Practice imagery geolocation: given a photograph with no EXIF data, use visible indicators (landmarks, shadows, flora, text, infrastructure) to narrow the location to a specific region, city, or street. Document your reasoning process.

## Source Evaluation and Reliability

**keywords**: source, evaluation, reliability, grading, corroboration, bias, deception, fabrication

### Facts
- Intelligence source grading systems (NATO standard): Source reliability rated A–F (A = completely reliable, through F = reliability cannot be judged); Information accuracy rated 1–6 (1 = confirmed, through 6 = truthfulness cannot be judged).
- Fabrication risk increases when: a source's reporting cannot be independently corroborated, a source has motivation to tell the collector what they want to hear, a source is under pressure (financial, coercive), or a source is aware of the collector's requirements.
- Cognitive bias in source evaluation: collectors who have established a relationship with a source tend to over-rate their reliability. The solution is structured evaluation by someone without a personal relationship to the source.
- The echo chamber failure: if all sources are drawn from the same community, all sources have the same access limitations and the same potential for shared error or shared deception.

### Procedures
- Reliability assessment protocol: maintain a source reliability record. For each piece of reporting, note whether it was later confirmed, contradicted, or never verified. Update reliability ratings based on track record, not personal relationship.
- Corroboration standard: no single-source intelligence should be used as the basis for a consequential action. Require corroboration from an independent source with independent access before acting on uncorroborated HUMINT.

### Exercises
- Apply the NATO two-letter grading system to five historical intelligence reports (use declassified case studies). For each, identify whether the original assessment was conservative, accurate, or overconfident in retrospect.
- Design a corroboration scheme for a specific operational scenario: identify three independent collection methods that could be used to corroborate a specific piece of HUMINT. For each method, identify its access limitations and what it could and could not confirm.
